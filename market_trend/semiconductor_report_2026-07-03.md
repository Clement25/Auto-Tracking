## Analysis & Outlook

**Post-Record Rally Correction Deepens Into Week 1 of Q3.** The semiconductor sector is digesting the consequences of one of the most extraordinary runs in financial market history. After the SOX index surged 87.8% in a single quarter — adding roughly $2 trillion in combined market cap across the largest chip names — the correction that began on July 1 is now entering its third session. Today's breadth (14 of 20 tracked stocks declining) confirms this is a sector-wide repricing rather than isolated stock-specific events. The severity of declines in semiconductor equipment (KLAC -11.51%, LRCX -10.19%, AMAT -7.35%) is particularly telling: equipment stocks amplify expectations about future wafer-start volumes and capex cycles, and the market is voting that near-term growth may be decelerating from peak expectations.

**Broadcom's Guidance Was the Detonator, But Not the Cause.** Broadcom's decision on June 3 to leave its full-year AI chip revenue target unchanged — rather than raise it — served as the proximate trigger for this correction, but the underlying pressure had been building. BofA's bubble risk indicator at 0.91, frothy valuations (AMD at 175x trailing earnings, AMAT and KLAC at 40x+ forward), and a Fed still unwilling to cut rates all created the conditions. Broadcom simply provided the excuse for profit-taking after a historic run. Importantly, Broadcom itself has been declining over the past month (down ~15%), reflecting that this is a structural re-rating of AI growth expectations rather than a single-session sentiment swing.

**Memory Remains the Structural Winner — But Even MU Isn't Immune.** Micron's ~260% YTD gain before this week's sell-off reflects the genuine scarcity of HBM3E supply in a world where NVIDIA's H100/H200/Blackwell systems all require prodigious memory bandwidth. The sell-off in MU (-5.49% today) does not negate the structural thesis: Micron's 2026 HBM production is fully contracted, and IDC projects DRAM revenues to nearly triple this year to $418.6 billion. Investors who bought MU at Jan 2026 prices are still sitting on massive gains even after this week's correction. The risk is if AI infrastructure investment cycles pause — something that SK Hynix's reported HBM moderation could signal.

**Divergences Signal Rotation, Not Sector Abandonment.** Today's recovery in MPWR (+7.96%), SWKS (+6.67%), and ENTG (+5.95%) — three names with diversified exposure beyond AI GPU infrastructure — is a healthy sign. Monolithic Power is being re-rated on the strength of data center power-delivery demand; Skyworks offers 5G smartphone content that is uncorrelated with AI capex; Entegris' process consumables are structural regardless of which chip architecture wins. These divergences suggest that sophisticated money is rotating within the sector rather than exiting altogether. Taiwan Semiconductor's relative strength (+0.52%) also matters: as the world's premier foundry, TSMC's customer base spans every major AI chip designer, giving it structural insulation.

**Forward Watch: Three Key Catalysts.** The next major event investors should monitor is Texas Instruments' Q2 2026 earnings on July 22 — TXN is the bellwether for the industrial and automotive semiconductor end markets, and its commentary on inventory digestion and order trends will set the tone for mid-cycle players. Second, any further guidance from hyperscalers (Microsoft, Amazon, Google, Meta) on their Q2/Q3 capex plans will either validate or further deflate the AI demand narrative. Third, the Federal Reserve's next policy decision will remain a macro anchor for high-multiple semiconductor valuations: with CPI still at 4.2% and the Bubble Risk Indicator at 0.91, any hint of rate hikes would likely accelerate the current correction. Despite this week's turbulence, the structural case for semiconductors in a $1.3 trillion annual market driven by generative AI, advanced packaging, and the next compute paradigm remains intact — the question for investors is simply about entry price and holding period.
