## Analysis & Outlook

The Computex 2026 conference in Taipei has become the semiconductor sector's defining event of the summer, and Jensen Huang once again proved himself the most market-moving CEO in technology. His orchestrated series of announcements — the Vera Rubin agentic AI CPU, the RTX Spark Windows PC superchip, an unprecedented $150 billion annual Taiwan investment pledge, and a live endorsement of Marvell as "the next trillion-dollar company" — collectively sent shockwaves across the entire chip ecosystem. Marvell's extraordinary 27% single-session surge illustrates the asymmetric power of the Jensen Huang endorsement effect: a public declaration from the CEO of the world's most valuable semiconductor company can add tens of billions in market cap in hours. The broader sector's strong performance — 13 of 20 tracked names advancing — reflects deepening institutional conviction that the AI infrastructure supercycle still has years of runway remaining.

The memory segment demands special attention as a sector within the sector. Micron Technology's historic climb past a $1 trillion market cap (May 26) represents a fundamental re-rating of DRAM's strategic value in the AI era. High-bandwidth memory (HBM) has become the single most constrained, highest-value component in AI accelerator systems, and with Micron as one of only three global HBM suppliers alongside SK Hynix and Samsung, the company can satisfy only 50–67% of customer demand. Gartner projects DRAM average selling prices rising 125% in 2026 — a pricing environment that should sustain Micron's extraordinary profitability well into fiscal 2027. The company's Q2 revenue of ~$24 billion (roughly triple the year-ago quarter) is not a one-quarter aberration; it reflects a structural inflection in memory economics driven by the mass deployment of AI accelerators.

TSMC's record valuation (>$2.28T market cap), intraday all-time highs, and planned 3nm pricing increases of up to 15% in H2 2026 underscore a seismic shift in fab economics. As NVIDIA, AMD, Google, AWS, and multiple cloud providers compete fiercely for advanced-node wafer allocation, TSMC has unambiguous and durable pricing power for the first time in a decade. Jensen Huang's $150B/year Taiwan investment commitment essentially guarantees sustained TSMC fab utilization through the end of the decade. The emerging constraint is not design capability but physical infrastructure: electricity capacity, copper wiring, specialty gases (helium, nitrogen trifluoride), and advanced packaging substrates are now genuine bottlenecks that TSMC — and the broader semiconductor supply chain — cannot resolve quickly through capital spending alone.

Qualcomm's -8.78% decline warrants careful monitoring as a signal of intensifying competitive dynamics. The company faces a genuine pincer movement in its core growth markets: NVIDIA's RTX Spark (100+ TOPS on-device AI compute) directly attacks the AI PC segment where QCOM's Snapdragon X Elite (45 TOPS) had been establishing early leadership. Meanwhile, Qualcomm's decision to unveil the "Dragonfly" data center AI chip brand at Computex without providing technical specifics — deferring all details to a June 24 Investor Day — frustrated investors who had bid the stock up ~27% the prior week on ByteDance chip deal excitement. The juxtaposition of QCOM's Computex tease and MRVL's Computex triumph illustrates a sector increasingly bifurcated between companies with clear, validated AI revenue trajectories and those still establishing their AI infrastructure credibility. Broadcom's Q2 earnings report tomorrow (June 3) will be the next major sector catalyst: consensus expects $22.11B in revenue (+47% YoY) and $2.40/share EPS (+52% YoY), and options are pricing a 10.65% move in either direction — a potential near-term volatility spike for the sector.

Looking ahead, divergence within the semiconductor sector is accelerating into a defining structural theme. Pure-play AI infrastructure names (NVDA, MRVL, TSM, AVGO, MU) continue to attract the overwhelming majority of institutional capital flows, while legacy analog, industrial, and non-AI end-market companies (TXN, MCHP, ENTG) face relative multiple compression. The KLAC 10-for-1 stock split (effective June 12) and AMAT's all-time high record results both signal that chip equipment manufacturers are seeing strong forward order books, which bodes well for the capex cycle through 2027. The primary risks to the sector's extraordinary momentum are: (1) escalating US-China trade policy uncertainty, particularly around advanced-node export controls that could impair NVIDIA, AMAT, and LRCX revenue in the China market; (2) physical infrastructure constraints limiting data center expansion timelines and delaying AI deployment; and (3) any indication from hyperscaler management teams that AI capex growth is approaching a plateau — which, given current projections of >50% growth in 2026, remains a low-probability but high-impact tail risk for the entire semiconductor ecosystem.
