### Sector-Wide

**Post-Selloff Recovery Underway**
The Philadelphia Semiconductor Index (SOXX) fell approximately 10% on June 6, 2026, wiping out over $1 trillion in semiconductor market value in a single session. The immediate trigger was Broadcom's (AVGO) Q3 AI chip sales guidance of $16 billion — below the $17.2 billion analyst consensus — along with management's decision not to raise its full-year 2026 AI semiconductor revenue forecast. The "sell-the-news" reaction spread across the entire chip supply chain. By June 11, the sector is staging a bifurcated recovery with markets broadly positive (Nasdaq +0.26%, S&P 500 +0.21%).

**Cantor Fitzgerald Equipment Upcycle Call**
Cantor Fitzgerald raised price targets on the three major US semiconductor equipment companies, citing a "multi-year supply-constrained and durable upcycle" with improved sector outlook: KLAC target raised from $2,000 to $2,500 (pre-split), AMAT from $575 to $650, LRCX from $320 to $425. The upgrades imply 16–31% upside from current levels.

According to Deloitte's 2026 semiconductor industry survey, 93% of sector leaders expect revenue growth this year, with AI cited as the top driver by 73% of respondents — followed by Cloud/Data Centers (61%), Wireless (57%), and Automotive (56%). The AI chip market alone is projected to reach approximately $500 billion in 2026. Importantly, tariffs and trade policy have emerged as the **#1 concern** among industry executives — surpassing supply chain risk for the first time, reflecting the new era of semiconductor geopolitics.

**Global Market Context**
On June 11, broader US equity markets edged higher as Iran signaled progress toward a deal with the US, easing some geopolitical pressure. ## Analysis & Outlook

The US semiconductor sector's June 11 session tells the story of a complex, bifurcated recovery following one of the sharpest single-day selloffs in the sector's recent history. The June 5-6 AVGO-triggered rout erased over $1 trillion in market value and forced a critical recalibration of expectations around AI chip demand velocity. Today's session provides important signal: the structural AI demand thesis remains intact, but the market has sharply distinguished between names with confirmed AI traction and those facing execution uncertainty or near-term guidance headwinds. Equipment makers and foundry beneficiaries are leading the recovery, while memory and certain platform chip names exhibit ongoing volatility.

The single most important development of June 11 is Intel's foundry breakthrough. Reports of Google and NVIDIA turning to Intel Foundry for AI chip manufacturing represent an extraordinary strategic validation of Intel's multi-year transformation — and carry implications well beyond INTC's 11% single-day gain. If confirmed at scale, this shift signals that the AI computing supply chain is beginning to diversify away from near-total TSMC dependency, with meaningful geopolitical and competitive consequences. TSMC's 4.48% decline today partially reflects this emerging narrative. For Intel bulls, the Google TPU order is a proof-of-concept moment; for the broader industry, it suggests the foundry landscape is entering a new competitive era that could sustain elevated capex across multiple equipment and materials suppliers for years.

Semiconductor capital equipment stocks continue to demonstrate the most durable fundamental underpinnings in the sector. Cantor Fitzgerald's "early innings of a multi-year supply-constrained upcycle" characterization for AMAT, LRCX, and KLAC is well-supported by data: AI accelerator demand requires unprecedented levels of advanced packaging, leading-edge logic, and memory capacity, all of which require sustained equipment investment cycles measured in years rather than quarters. KLA's 10-for-1 stock split — reflecting 149.9% appreciation over twelve months and JP Morgan's forecast for earnings to triple by 2030 — is a powerful statement about the long-duration nature of this investment cycle. ASML's crossing of the $700B market cap threshold further underscores the structural demand for EUV lithography that enables every advanced chip being designed today.

The primary near-term risk is the question Broadcom raised but didn't answer: is AI chip spending at the hyperscaler level beginning to moderate? AVGO's guidance shortfall was the first crack in the near-universal assumption of relentless AI capex escalation. While the company's custom silicon business remains structurally sound — hyperscalers are still designing and ordering their own AI ASICs at scale — the market's violent reaction suggests that any deviation from exponential growth trajectories will be severely punished. The secondary risk is macro and geopolitical: tariffs and trade policy are now the #1 industry concern per Deloitte, and Iran-related geopolitical tensions (cited in today's market commentary) add to the general uncertainty. ASML's EUV export restrictions to China, potential equipment tariffs, and supply chain vulnerability to Taiwan Strait risk are all live concerns that investors must price.

Looking at stocks to watch heading into the second half of June: **AMAT** is approaching its 52-week high with strong analyst support and fundamental momentum — a decisive breakout above $557.62 would be technically and thematically significant. **INTC** is perhaps the most dynamic setup in the sector, as the foundry inflection story is still in its earliest chapters and the stock trades at a fraction of where it would need to be to fully reflect a successful foundry transformation. **NVDA's** dividend increase to $0.25/quarter is a shareholder confidence signal rarely seen at a $4.99T market cap company, but the stock faces scrutiny at the convergence of peak valuation and nascent demand moderation fears. **MU** warrants close attention as any weakness in HBM pricing data or softer forward guidance would be a critical test of the memory bull thesis. Finally, **MRVL's** S&P 500 inclusion and record data center revenue make it a potential relative outperformer as index-buying demand compounds fundamental AI tailwinds.
