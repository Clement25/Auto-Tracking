**DRAM Antitrust Lawsuit — Major Near-Term Catalyst**  
A class-action suit filed June 25 in the U.S. District Court for the Northern District of California (Judge Noel Wise) names Samsung, SK Hynix, and Micron as defendants, alleging the three companies violated Section 1 of the Sherman Act by deliberately shifting production capacity from commodity DRAM to high-margin HBM for AI servers, artificially constraining supply and pushing DRAM prices up ~700% over four years. Micron denied the allegations, calling its conduct "vigorous, fair, and lawful." The suit triggered a wave of selling across memory stocks globally, with Samsung and SK Hynix shares also tumbling sharply in Seoul. ## Analysis & Outlook

### Q3 Opens with a Reckoning After Record H1 2026

The US semiconductor sector entered the third quarter in full retreat, as profit-taking overwhelmed what had been one of the most exceptional stretches of performance in the industry's history. Through June 30, 2026, AMD had surged 142% year-to-date, Intel an astonishing 233%, and Micron had ascended from a sub-$104 52-week low to an all-time high of $1,255 — a journey that encapsulates the AI-driven memory supercycle in a single data point. The Philadelphia Semiconductor Index logged records through late June as hyperscaler AI infrastructure spending translated directly into silicon demand. Yet the very scale of these gains created the conditions for a sharp reversal: stretched valuations, crowded positioning, and the need for any negative catalyst to trigger forced selling.

### Twin Catalysts: Lawsuit and Demand Visibility Concerns

The Q3 opening selloff was lit by two specific spark plugs. The more structurally significant was a class-action antitrust suit filed June 25 in a California federal court, naming Samsung Electronics, SK Hynix, and Micron as defendants in an alleged scheme to artificially constrain commodity DRAM supply while pivoting capacity to high-margin HBM — leading to a ~700% increase in DRAM prices over four years. While antitrust litigation in the semiconductor space is historically slow and outcomes uncertain, the suit strikes at the heart of the AI memory trade thesis: that memory suppliers hold durable pricing power. The second catalyst was a report that Meta Platforms was exploring renting out excess AI compute capacity — an early signal that cloud AI supply may be catching up to demand, raising questions about whether hyperscaler capex will continue its breakneck pace. Taken together, these two developments forced investors to ask a question markets had been reluctant to pose through H1: what if the AI monetization cycle takes longer than the semiconductor cycle has already priced in?

### Equipment Makers Bear the Brunt; Intel Bucks the Trend

Within the sector, semiconductor equipment companies — KLA (-11.51%) and Applied Materials (-9.97%) — absorbed disproportionate selling pressure. This is partly mechanical: equipment stocks trade at a forward multiplier to chip company revenues, so any downward revision to end-demand expectations hits equipment stocks harder through the operating leverage channel. KLA's upcoming July 28 earnings report will be the sector's most closely watched datapoint heading into Q3: if management guides conservatively on equipment orders, it will confirm the market's concern about a late-2026 capex digestion cycle. By contrast, Intel defied the bearish narrative, with HSBC setting Wall Street's most bullish target at $200 — double its prior target — citing Intel Foundry's growing customer pipeline, including Apple, Terafab, and potential NVIDIA and Google engagements. Having started 2026 near $36, INTC's 233% YTD surge reflects a genuine rehabilitation story, and the HSBC thesis suggests the foundry valuation unlock has only begun.

### Memory's Paradox: HBM Boom Meets Legal Headwinds

Micron's journey from $103 to $1,255 and back toward $975 in a matter of weeks illustrates the volatility embedded in hyper-cyclical AI memory plays. Total DRAM revenues are forecast by IDC to reach $418.6 billion in 2026 (+177% YoY), with HBM for AI servers the primary driver. Micron, Samsung, and SK Hynix have been the primary beneficiaries. Yet the antitrust suit introduces a scenario in which this extraordinary pricing power becomes the legal liability itself — plaintiffs allege the trio conspired to make conventional DRAM artificially scarce, inflating prices by hundreds of percent. Even a moderate settlement or behavioral remedies could structurally cap HBM pricing dynamics. Investors should watch Q3 earnings guidance closely for any early signs of demand softening or litigation-related provisioning. The 52-week low-to-high spread of $103.38–$1,255.00 in MU remains one of the most remarkable single-name moves in semiconductor equity history.

### Outlook: Structural Bull Case Intact, Near-Term Uncertainty Elevated

Despite the sharp Q3 opening, the fundamental case for semiconductor equities over a multi-year horizon remains compelling. IDC forecasts the global semiconductor market to cross $1.29 trillion in 2026 (+52.8% YoY), with AI chips constituting the majority of revenue growth for the first time ever. Taiwan Semiconductor's $2.25T market cap — more than double the GDP of many nations — reflects the market's confidence in TSMC's irreplaceable role in the AI compute supply chain. Broadcom's dominant AI ASIC and networking franchise, AMD's continued server CPU share gains, and Marvell's custom silicon design wins represent the sector's structural winners. Key risks to monitor include: (1) slower-than-expected AI ROI realization by hyperscalers leading to capex discipline; (2) new DRAM/HBM oversupply cycle should South Korean fabs accelerate expansion; (3) geopolitical risk around Taiwan and US-China chip export controls. Stocks to watch into Q3: **INTC** on Intel Foundry monetization milestones; **MRVL** on hyperscaler custom silicon design award announcements; **AVGO** as a potential value reentry ~14% below its 2026 highs; and **KLAC** on July 28 earnings as the equipment sector's canary.
