## Analysis & Outlook

**The AI Supercycle Is Not a Rumor — It's a P&L Event**  
The 2026 semiconductor rally stands apart from prior cycles because it is grounded in verifiable, accelerating revenue. Micron's 196% quarterly revenue surge, Broadcom's AI revenues doubling year-over-year, and TSMC running at near-full capacity for leading-edge nodes are not projections — they are reported results. The SIA-Deloitte forecast of $1.2 trillion in AI data center chip revenue by 2028 implies the industry is less than 15% of the way through the buildout. Companies that own the critical bottlenecks — HBM (Micron), advanced packaging (TSMC, Intel), custom ASICs (Broadcom, Marvell), and wafer fab equipment (AMAT, LRCX, KLAC) — command premium valuations that are, for now, justified by unit economics.

**Computex 2026: A Catalyst, Not a Peak**  
This week's Computex conference injected a wave of CEO-level endorsements and product announcements that accelerated multiple re-ratings. Jensen Huang's Marvell call is the most dramatic example: the +32.5% single-day move reflects not just enthusiasm but a fundamental revision of Marvell's total addressable market as custom AI silicon displaces merchant GPU deployments at the margin. Intel's Computex showing — combined with its 250% YTD stock performance — signals that the market is beginning to price in a genuine foundry and AI infrastructure turnaround under CEO Lip-Bu Tan. The key catalysts in the near term are Broadcom's Q2 results (today), Qualcomm's Investor Day (June 24), and any HBM4 production updates from Micron and SK Hynix.

**Stocks to Watch: Custom Silicon, Memory, and Equipment**  
Three thematic pockets stand out. First, **custom silicon** — Broadcom (AVGO) and Marvell (MRVL) are the clear leaders; Qualcomm's ByteDance ASIC deal opens a new revenue line and could be transformative if it scales. Second, **memory** — Micron (MU) remains the most underappreciated name in the sector given its HBM dominance, explosive margin expansion, and trillion-dollar milestone; the guidance trajectory points to continued outperformance. Third, **wafer fab equipment** — AMAT, LRCX, and KLAC continue to set 52-week highs as semiconductor fabs globally accelerate capacity investments; KLAC's 10-for-1 split signals management confidence and could attract a new class of retail investors post-split.

**Risks: Valuations, Geopolitics, and Power Infrastructure**  
The single largest risk is valuation compression if AI hyperscaler capex guidance is revised downward — a scenario that seems distant today but could emerge in H2 2026 as capex cycles normalize. Geopolitical risk around Taiwan remains a persistent overhang for TSM and ASML specifically. Structural bottlenecks — HBM availability, advanced packaging throughput, and power infrastructure — are already creating delivery delays; any supply-side shock could rapidly shift sentiment. The comparison to dot-com valuations made by some strategists warrants attention: while the revenue reality is far more robust than 1999, the compressed timeline between innovation cycles leaves less margin for execution error. Selective profit-taking on the most momentum-stretched names (MRVL +225% YTD, INTC +250% YTD, MU +300%+ YTD) is prudent.

**Forward-Looking: Sector Remains in Structural Bull Cycle**  
The secular tailwinds — AI model scaling, sovereign AI buildouts, edge inference deployment, and next-generation HBM4 ramps — are measured in years, not quarters. While near-term consolidation after a +65% SOX YTD move is entirely plausible, the structural case for semiconductor exposure remains intact. Broadcom's earnings tonight serve as the next major sector read-through; a beat-and-raise from the AI ASIC leader would extend the rally and set a positive tone for the summer. Investors should monitor AMAT, LRCX, and KLAC for wafer equipment order momentum as the clearest leading indicator of the cycle's durability.
