## Analysis & Outlook

**Sector Recovery in Motion — But Differentiation Increasing**

The US semiconductor sector is in the midst of a post-correction recovery following the sharpest drawdown since early 2024. Between late June and early July 2026, names like Micron fell ~32%, Broadcom slid ~24% below its 52-week high, and the SOX index lost significant ground — all triggered by a combination of macroeconomic uncertainty, fears that AI capital spending returns were insufficient, and an open-source model release from Chinese startup Moonshot (Kimi K3) that momentarily reignited demand-destruction fears. However, the July 21 rebound — with the SOX surging ~5% and MU gaining 12% in a single session — signals that the selloff was likely overdone and that fundamentals remain broadly intact.

**AI Demand Is the Anchor, Not the Risk**

Multiple confirming data points reinforce that AI-driven chip demand is structural, not speculative. ASML's Q2 beats and raised guidance indicate customers are still accelerating capacity expansion. Micron's data center revenue doubling YoY and record HBM shipments confirm hyperscaler AI infrastructure spending has not paused. Broadcom's AI semiconductor revenue growing 143% YoY underscores the breadth of the AI build-out. These are not lagging indicators — ASML's book-to-bill and Micron's quarterly guidance are forward-looking signals that suggest Q3 and Q4 2026 remain on an upward trajectory for the sector.

**Intel Earnings: The Wildcard for the Week**

Thursday's Intel Q2 earnings (July 23) are the most significant near-term catalyst for the sector. Intel has lagged peers dramatically in the AI compute race, but recent restructuring (including data center layoffs), cost discipline, and renewed 18A process node narrative have rekindled speculative interest. The stock's 8.64% surge on July 21 reflects options market positioning and short covering rather than fundamental conviction. Post-earnings volatility is expected to be high, and the magnitude of the move may set the tone for the broader sector through month-end.

**Stocks to Watch**

MRVL and LRCX stand out as high-conviction recovery candidates. Marvell has emerged as a key pure-play on custom AI silicon (ASICs for hyperscalers) and optical interconnects, with revenue estimates being revised upward even as the stock remains well below recent peaks. Lam Research, as a wafer fab equipment supplier, is a leveraged proxy for the memory capex cycle that Micron's results just confirmed is accelerating. AVGO is another compelling name, trading ~24% below its 52-week high despite nearly tripling its AI revenue on a YoY basis — the market appears to be pricing in a semiconductor slowdown that the company's own results explicitly contradict.

**Risks and Key Uncertainties**

Not all risks have dissipated. The divergence between Intel (declining on July 22 after a brief surge) and names like AVGO (continuing to advance) suggests the market is becoming more selective. Companies tied to legacy PC and automotive end markets — INTC, MCHP, ON, SWKS — face structurally weaker near-term demand than pure AI-infrastructure names. Skyworks, in particular, continues to languish 14% below its 52-week high as smartphone demand normalization outweighs any AI tailwind. Geopolitical risk around Taiwan (TSM), and the ongoing debate over US export controls to China, remain overhanging macro tail risks that could reverse the sector recovery on any escalation. Finally, Morgan Stanley's cautious stance — warning of a "hard remainder of 2026" — should not be dismissed; if hyperscaler capex guidance softens at any Q3 earnings release, the sector could re-test July lows quickly.

**Bottom Line**

The semiconductor sector is in a fundamentally sound but technically volatile period. The weight of evidence — ASML order book, Micron HBM demand, Broadcom AI revenue growth, and TSM record revenue — points to a continuing AI infrastructure build-out that should support sector earnings through at least 2026. The sell-off was a reset of stretched valuations rather than a signal of structural demand destruction. Investors with a 6–12 month horizon may find current price levels attractive entry points in AI-pure-play names (NVDA, AVGO, MRVL, LRCX, MU). Near-term traders should watch Intel earnings July 23 and major tech earnings from Alphabet and Tesla — which will provide a read on correlation between AI spend and revenue returns — as the sector's next key inflection points.
