## Analysis & Outlook

**The AI Supercycle Is Real — and Broadening.** Heading into mid-2026, the US semiconductor sector is in the middle of the most powerful structural bull market in its history. The SOXQ ETF is up 99% year-to-date and Gartner projects total semiconductor revenue will grow 64% to exceed $1.3 trillion in 2026. This is not a narrow NVIDIA story anymore: Micron has tripled in price on memory supercycle dynamics, Taiwan Semiconductor has gained 46% on the back of diversified hyperscaler partnerships, and ON Semiconductor has doubled from its 2025 cyclical nadir. AI’s demand for compute — training models, scaling inference, and building out edge AI — is consuming silicon at a pace the industry has never seen, and the supply chain is only beginning to catch up.

**Memory Is the Most Compelling Near-Term Catalyst.** Micron Technology’s June 24 earnings are the single most important event for the sector this week. HBM capacity is completely sold out through 2026 under multi-year customer contracts, and Micron’s guidance of $33.5B in revenue with ~81% non-GAAP gross margins (vs. 39% a year ago) illustrates the dramatic pricing shift underway. Gartner estimates DRAM prices will rise 125% in 2026. This “memflation” is not just a windfall for Micron — it signals that AI infrastructure buildout is genuinely consuming all available advanced memory capacity, lending credibility to the thesis that the supercycle is structural, not cyclical.

**Intel’s Foundry Revival and the Competitive Reshaping of Logic.** Intel’s +10.65% single-day surge on the Alphabet foundry win is significant beyond the revenue impact. The deal validates Intel 18A’s process node capabilities and breaks the market’s assumption that TSMC is the only viable option for advanced logic chips. With only 33% of analysts currently bullish on Intel, the stock carries meaningful contrarian upside if further hyperscaler design wins emerge. Meanwhile, Marvell Technology’s NVIDIA partnership and upcoming S&P 500 inclusion signal the rapid rise of custom ASICs as a complement — and partial alternative — to GPU-based computing. MRVL’s 287% one-year return has been built on real revenue catalysts, not speculation.

**The Broadcom Wobble: A Speed Bump, Not a Reversal.** The sector’s biggest scare of the month came from Broadcom, whose Q3 AI revenue guidance of $16B disappointed against the $17.2B consensus, and whose full-year $56B AI chip outlook remained unchanged. The stock fell 12.6% in a single session, dragging the SOXX down 2.1% and wiping roughly $1 trillion in sector market value within days. However, context matters: Broadcom’s Q2 AI revenue was $10.8B (+143% YoY), and the $56B full-year target still represents extraordinary growth. The market interpreted the unchanged guidance as a ceiling; management appeared to view it as a floor. Stocks across the sector have since recovered sharply, with the rebound validating that the sell-off was a sentiment event rather than a fundamental deterioration.

**Risks and Divergences to Watch.** Not all semiconductors benefit equally from the AI boom. Skyworks Solutions (-5.6% recently), with heavy exposure to smartphone RF chips, is trading 14% below its 52-week high as consumer electronics demand remains tepid. Entegris faces uncertainty from evolving US export restrictions on advanced semiconductor materials and chemicals for China. Meanwhile, Qualcomm is navigating a pivot toward AI edge computing to reduce smartphone concentration risk. Equipment makers — AMAT, LRCX, KLAC — are surging on AI fab capex commitments, but are acutely sensitive to any China-related WFE spending restrictions. The forward question for the sector is whether AI-related demand can sustain momentum through 2027 and into the next planning cycle, or whether the extraordinary YTD gains have pulled forward multiple years of returns. Micron’s June 24 earnings and any signals on 2027 HBM contract visibility will be a critical gating factor.
