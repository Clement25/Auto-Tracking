## Analysis & Outlook

Today’s sell-off represents the semiconductor sector’s most severe single-session drawdown in weeks, and it arrived on two converging forces: a hawkish macro shock and structural AI-valuation anxiety. Bank of America’s note warning of up to three Federal Reserve rate hikes in 2026 under new Chair Kevin Warsh struck directly at the high-multiple valuations the sector has been trading at. With AI-themed chip stocks having already traded at a premium to the broader technology sector, the prospect of persistently higher interest rates — which compress discounted cash flow valuations — is particularly dangerous. The VanEck SMH ETF’s 6.5% decline and the KOSPI’s 10% plunge confirmed this was a global repricing event, not a company-specific or U.S.-only phenomenon.

Beneath the macro pressure lies a sector-specific anxiety that has been simmering since Broadcom’s June 3–4 earnings disappointment: are hyperscalers actually consuming AI chips at the rate the industry had assumed? The four largest cloud platforms are projected to deploy $630B in infrastructure spend in 2026, but investors are asking whether debt-financed capex at that scale is sustainable — especially with the Federal Reserve tightening the cost of capital. Broadcom’s $16B AI chip sales guide (vs. $17.2B expected) was the first crack; today’s global sell-off may be the market stress-testing that thesis more broadly. Crucially, none of the underlying AI adoption trends have changed — Gartner still forecasts $1.3T in total semiconductor revenue for 2026, and DRAM revenues are on track for a historic $418.6B on HBM demand alone — but the market is clearly marking down the “peak cycle” probability.

The equipment sub-sector deserves particular attention as a forward indicator. AMAT, LRCX, and KLAC all fell between 8.77% and 9.44% today, moving in near-lockstep — a signal that institutional investors are reducing their semiconductor equipment exposure en masse. Equipment orders are placed 6–18 months before chip production, so today’s repricing reflects concern about what happens to 2027 fab capacity additions if hyperscaler AI spending decelerates. Applied Materials’ move from its 52-week high of $641.18 (essentially yesterday’s close) to $584 in a single session is a powerful reminder of how quickly equipment valuations can reverse when cycle assumptions shift.

Marvell’s -14.71% decline is the session’s most instructive data point. MRVL had been the 2026 bull market’s poster child — up ~265% YTD on custom ASIC wins, record revenues, and a freshly-minted S&P 500 slot — and today’s reversal reflects how violently crowded positions unwind when macro sentiment turns. This does not invalidate Marvell’s AI infrastructure thesis: its Teralynx switch chips, hyperscaler custom compute wins, and a multi-year revenue ramp from $2.4B toward $2.7B+ per quarter remain in place. However, traders who had been long MRVL for the S&P 500 inclusion event have now rotated out, and valuation consolidation may take time.

Looking forward, the immediate focus is Micron’s fiscal Q3 earnings on June 24. MU had set an all-time high of $1,213.56 on June 22 — just 24 hours before shedding 11% — making the earnings reaction a high-stakes event for the entire sector. If Micron delivers on the $20.20 EPS estimate and provides confident Q4 HBM shipment guidance, it could arrest the broader sell-off and validate the AI memory supercycle. Conversely, any softness in pricing outlook or inventory caution would deepen the retreat. The two stocks that held positive today — Skyworks (+5.15%) and Microchip Technology (+2.95%) — are notable precisely because they sit outside the hyperscaler AI orbit. Their outperformance suggests the market is not abandoning semiconductors broadly, but is selectively deflating the AI premium that accumulated over the past several months. In that sense, today’s sell-off may prove healthy rather than catastrophic — a necessary reset of expectations before the sector’s next leg higher.
