Operating margin hit a record 67%. Despite guiding Q3 AI revenue toward $16 billion (expected to triple QoQ) and reiterating $100B+ in FY2027 AI semiconductor revenue, the stock dropped 12.6% on Thursday and an additional 6.3% on Friday. Market reaction reflects how elevated expectations—built on a stock that had nearly doubled from its 52-week low—required perfection and beyond. With 44 of 47 analysts rating AVGO a Buy, the sell reaction was almost entirely about positioning, not fundamentals. CEO Hock Tan noted six hyperscaler custom chip customers including Anthropic, Google, Meta, and OpenAI driving AI revenue growth.

## Analysis & Outlook

### The Broadcom Paradox: When Great Results Aren't Good Enough

The semiconductor sector's two-day rout was initiated by one of the more paradoxical earnings reactions in recent memory. Broadcom delivered objectively exceptional fiscal Q2 results—AI revenue doubling to $10.8 billion, total revenue up 48% YoY, record operating margins of 67%, and forward guidance calling for AI revenue of $16 billion in Q3 (nearly tripling sequentially). In any prior market cycle, such results would have sent the stock to new highs. Instead, AVGO plunged 18% over Thursday and Friday. The reaction encapsulates the fundamental tension facing the entire AI semiconductor complex in mid-2026: valuations have run so far ahead of even spectacular execution that the market's tolerance for anything less than a step-function upside surprise has been exhausted. Broadcom's stock had traded within striking distance of $500—up nearly 100% from its 52-week low—embedding a version of the AI revenue future that only the most aggressive sell-side models had modeled. When reality proved strong-but-not-extraordinary, the air came out. This dynamic is not unique to AVGO; it is the operating reality for every high-multiple semiconductor name in 2026.

### Macro Crosscurrents: When Strength Becomes Weakness

The selloff was amplified by an unusual macro dynamic on June 5: the May non-farm payrolls report came in stronger than expected at 172,000 jobs with unemployment stable at 4.3%. In a normal market environment, this would signal economic health and be modestly constructive for cyclical semiconductor demand. But in a rate-sensitive equity market still digesting the Federal Reserve's "higher for longer" posture, robust employment data reduces the probability of near-term rate cuts—and high-multiple growth stocks like semiconductor names priced at 25–40x forward earnings are acutely sensitive to discount rate shifts. Treasury yields spiked, the dollar strengthened, and the resulting liquidity rotation out of technology was immediate. The Nasdaq fell approximately 600 points (roughly -3.5%), with semiconductors experiencing the steepest intraday declines. This macro overlay transformed what might have been a contained AVGO-specific selloff into a sector-wide event.

### The Structural AI Bull Case Remains Unbroken

Critically, the events of this week do not alter the fundamental case for AI-driven semiconductor demand. TSMC management confirmed that AI-driven chip demand "continues to exceed expectations"—a remarkable statement for what is already a historically elevated growth rate. IDC's projection of $1.29 trillion in global semiconductor revenues for 2026 (up 52.8% YoY) is driven by an AI infrastructure buildout that shows no signs of decelerating. The $32.6 billion HBM memory market expected in 2026 (versus $15.2 billion just two years earlier) has created an extraordinary windfall for Micron, which hit a $1 trillion market cap milestone before this week's pullback. Marvell's strong quarterly results and multi-year hyperscaler design wins (Amazon, Microsoft, Google) demonstrate that AI silicon demand is broadening beyond NVIDIA's GPU-centric architecture into custom XPUs, networking silicon, and optical interconnects—a diversification of the AI chip opportunity that ultimately benefits the entire ecosystem. Jensen Huang's Computex commitment to expand Taiwan investment is an irreversible, multi-year capex commitment that validates TSMC's manufacturing stranglehold on leading-edge AI silicon.

### Stocks to Watch: Differentiation Within the Selloff

The week's selloff creates differentiated opportunities. Marvell (MRVL) deserves close attention: after surrendering ~10.3% Friday following a +130% YTD run, it remains one of the highest-quality AI chip franchises in the market, with hyperscaler customer lock-in and FY2028 revenue guidance of $15 billion providing a long-term floor. Monday's price action will be revealing. TSMC (TSM), down only 3.87% Friday versus the sector's 5–10% declines, continues to demonstrate its position as the sector's "landlord"—it wins regardless of which chip architecture dominates, charges above-market margins, and just announced a 15% 3nm price hike signaling it holds all pricing power. KLA Corporation (KLAC), trading near $1,978 ahead of its June 12 10-for-1 stock split, and Applied Materials (AMAT, $474.19) offer exposure to the semiconductor equipment cycle that is structurally tied to capex regardless of AI vs. non-AI chip mix. For more value-oriented positioning, Intel (INTC) at $103—up ~440% from its 52-week low of $18.97—presents a complex risk/reward given its foundry ambitions, but the stock's recovery trajectory and manufacturing investment pipeline make it harder to dismiss than it was 12 months ago.

### Risks and Forward Catalysts

The primary near-term risk for the sector is the "expectations overhang": as AI chip stocks approach near-perfect execution requirements, any moderation in hyperscaler capex guidance—from Microsoft, Google, Amazon, or Meta—could trigger a more sustained repricing. A secondary risk is geopolitical. ASML's EUV lithography monopoly, TSMC's Taiwan concentration, and the ongoing US-China technology export framework all represent tail risks that can surface rapidly in risk-off environments. The CHIPS Act funding timelines and Intel's domestic foundry buildout add regulatory complexity. Looking ahead, the sector's key catalysts in the near term include TSMC's June monthly revenue report (expected mid-month), which will test whether AI wafer demand held up in Q2, and the KLAC post-split trading debut on June 12. Beyond that, Nvidia's August earnings cycle and the pace of Broadcom's Q3 AI revenue ramp (guided to $16 billion) will be the defining data points of the summer. Friday's selloff—painful as it is—remains more consistent with a technical breather within an intact secular AI semiconductor bull market than a structural trend reversal.
